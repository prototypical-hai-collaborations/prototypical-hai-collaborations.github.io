Prototypical Human-AI Collaboration Behaviors from LLM-Assisted Writing in the Wild

Sheshera Mysore1, Debarati Das2, Hancheng Cao1, Bahar Sarrafzadeh1
1Office of Applied Research, Microsoft
2University of Minnesota
Paper arXiv Code Data Thread

Overview

What does it mean to collaborate with LLM-powered Copilots? A common metaphor is to think of LLMs as “assistants” – users supply a prompt specifying their task, the LLM responds, and users leave with the response in hand. Instead, we shifted our metaphor to LLMs as “collaborators” and asked what it means to collaborate with LLM Copilots. We answered this by analyzing English multi-turn sessions from 21 million Microsoft Bing Copilot and public log datasets, which capture user-LLM interactions.

What we did

Recent papers have taken the “assistant” metaphor and examined the tasks users engage in with Copilots such as Claude, Bing Copilot, and WildChat (e.g. coding, search, writing, etc). Instead, we take the LLMs as a “collaborator” metaphor and analyze users' follow-ups after making an initial request because they capture how users collaborate with the LLM. We ran our analysis only on writing tasks to keep the analysis manageable.

We asked two questions in our analysis: (Q1) What user behaviors emerge in user-LLM collaborations? (Q2) Do users collaborate differently depending on their writing intent, e.g., differing in behavior while writing an email vs writing a story?

We addressed the first question by clustering users’ follow-ups and identified a small set of clusters that represent prototypical human-AI collaboration behaviors. We’ll refer to them as PATHs. Then, we ran a regression analysis between writing intents and PATHs to answer our second question.

What we found

We found that eight PATHs explained 80-85% of the variance in our large-scale log datasets. These PATHs corresponded to users asking questions, requesting more responses, and adding large chunks of text to the response, among others. Our paper describes the complete set of PATHs.

Most interestingly, we found a strong correlation between specific writing intents and PATHs. For example, users frequently asked for more responses when they were brainstorming. Similarly, they asked questions as they wrote technical or professional documents – showing how users brought together tasks that researchers historically treated as separate from one another (search and writing). When users wrote texts that they wanted to personalize, they added large chunks of text to it – for example, a story they wanted creative control over or a personal speech. Our paper describes several such correlations and highlights under-explored research problems that warrant further exploration in future work.

What this means for LLM alignment

Our results have implications for session-level LLM alignment and alignment under uncertain or evolving feedback, but we'll highlight a single key implication here – LLMs as "thought partners."

In asking questions, seeking feedback, and brainstorming with LLMs, users treated LLMs as partners in longer-term learning. Aligning LLMs for long-term learning represents a compelling opportunity, and we echo recent calls to develop LLMs as thought partners. This goal will require that alignment research prevents the homogenization of human voices and ideas and incorporates some of the desirable difficulties known to be crucial for learning.

Our paper discusses all the details and cites several recent papers in line with our findings and implications. Please take a look!
